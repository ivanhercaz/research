Why Creative Commons?

Digital technologies are in a constant tension with the copyright legislations. While Internet and another new technologies make easier the ways to share, reuse and remix the creative works, copyright legislation restrict this capabilities.

Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act

The battle for the extension of Public Domain, by Christopher Dombrus (CC BY-SA 4.0) in Wikimedia Commons

Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act

CTEA is the accronym used to refer to this act, in which the Government of the United States plus 20 years more to term of the copyright, that is a 70 years.

As interesting fact, this act is referred frequently as Mickey Mouse Copyright Law, because Disney Corp. is an advocate for the hardening of the term of copyright.

Eldred vs Ahscroft

Professor Lawrence Lessig of the Stanford Law School considered this act as unconstitutional. He represented to Eric Eldred, a web publisher of public domain works. They challenged this act in the U. S. Supreme Court, because it violated the own constitution.

Tom Bell's graph, vectorized by Clorox (CC BY-SA 3.0), in Wikimedia Commons

They lost the case

But then, they and Hal Abeson founded in 2001 Creative Commons, a non profit organisation with the scope of allow the creators to share their creative works on more flexible terms than Copyright.

Set of licenses

In 2002, Creative Commons launch the first set of free and public licenses. Nowadays, this set is composed by licenses in a freedom scale.

Freedom scale (without CC0) by Foter (CC BY-SA 3.0) in Wikimedia Commons

Organization

CC is a nonprofit and distributed organization with staff around the world. In 2016 the organization began a new organization strategy based on two keys:

  • The machine, composed by the legal tools designed and stewarded by CC.
  • Supporting the movement. CC helps the open movement, training and empowering advocates, and working directly with the community.

Movement and CC Global Network

Movement and CC Global Network

Over the years, many and diverse persons has been formed on Creative Commons open licensing. It is not necessary to be formally connected to be part of the movement, because anyone can be an advocate for open culture, either creating or sharing.

In addition, there is a formal group too, CC Global Network, in which there is organized areas of work called "Network Platforms". Anyone can participate in them. This formal group is only a piece betweem many: Wikimedians, Mozilians, open source and open access advocates, among other groups.

Free licenses are a global necessity with a global movement in which anyone can collaborate, as creator or users of the content
